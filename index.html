Reimagining Copyright at the Intersection of Creativity, Transformation, and Law

In the contemporary digital landscape, creativity increasingly emerges through reinterpretation, remixing, and transformation of pre-existing works. Digital platforms and participatory culture have reshaped how art is produced, consumed, and circulated — creativity no longer exists as isolated authorship but as part of a shared and constantly evolving cultural ecosystem.

Central Question

When does creative borrowing become infringement, and when does it constitute legitimate transformation?

01 — About

About This Project

This project undertakes a critical examination of copyright law in the context of contemporary remix culture. Traditional copyright frameworks are based on the protection of original expression and grant exclusive rights to creators as an incentive for innovation and economic reward. However, modern artistic practices increasingly rely on adaptation, recombination, and transformation of existing material.

The project analyses core legal doctrines such as originality, fair dealing, and the idea–expression dichotomy, while situating them within real-world creative practices. It further evaluates how effectively these doctrines respond to technological developments including digital reproduction, artificial intelligence, and global content circulation.

By combining doctrinal analysis, case law, and industry examples, the project assesses whether copyright law successfully balances the protection of creators with the promotion of cultural and technological progress.

02 — Key Concepts

Legal Framework & Doctrine

01
Originality

Copyright protection requires originality, traditionally understood as the application of skill, labour, and judgment. In a digital environment where creativity often builds upon existing works, originality increasingly involves transformation rather than independent creation.

02
Idea–Expression Dichotomy

Copyright protects the expression of ideas but not the ideas themselves. While this distinction appears clear in theory, it is often difficult to apply in practice, especially in creative works where themes, structures, and stylistic elements overlap.

03
Substantial Similarity

Infringement is determined by whether a substantial part of a work has been copied. Courts assess both qualitative and quantitative similarity, often relying on the perception of an ordinary observer rather than purely technical analysis.

04
Derivative Works

Derivative works are based on pre-existing material and involve adaptation or modification. The legal challenge lies in distinguishing legitimate transformation from unauthorized reproduction.

05
Fair Use (United States)

Fair use is a flexible doctrine that allows unauthorized use based on a four-factor test, with particular emphasis on whether the use is transformative and whether it harms the market for the original work.

06
Fair Dealing (India & UK)

Fair dealing permits use only for specific statutory purposes such as research, criticism, review, and reporting of current events. It offers limited flexibility and restricts judicial discretion compared to the American approach.

07
Transformative Use

Transformative use evaluates whether a work adds new meaning, expression, or purpose. While central to US copyright law, this doctrine is not formally recognised in Indian law, creating a significant doctrinal gap.

03 — Indian Copyright Framework

Copyright Act, 1957

Under the Copyright Act, 1957, copyright law in India provides a structured system of protection for creators. The Act establishes a comprehensive framework governing creation, ownership, and enforcement of copyright in literary, artistic, musical, and cinematographic works.

A defining feature of Indian copyright law is its reliance on fair dealing rather than fair use. Unlike the flexible American approach, Indian law restricts permissible use to specific categories, significantly limiting the scope for creative reinterpretation.

Indian courts have consistently held that commercial intent negates claims of fair dealing. Unauthorized use of copyrighted material for profit is almost always treated as infringement. This has significant implications for music sampling and remix culture.

In practice, creators must obtain licences from both the owner of the composition and the owner of the sound recording. The absence of a recognised transformative use doctrine and the lack of a clear de minimis exception make the legal environment particularly restrictive.

"The Indian framework prioritises certainty and rights-holder protection over creative flexibility — a tension that grows sharper with each technological development."

Section 14 — Exclusive Rights
Defines the exclusive rights of copyright holders, including reproduction, distribution, public performance, and adaptation of the protected work.
Section 52 — Fair Dealing Exceptions
Sets out fair dealing exceptions for purposes such as research, criticism, review, and reporting of current events. Closed list — no judicial expansion permitted.
Section 57 — Moral Rights
Protects the moral rights of authors, ensuring that their work is not distorted or modified in a manner that harms their reputation, independently of economic rights.
Duration of Protection
Copyright protection extends to the life of the author plus sixty years, aligning with the Berne Convention's minimum standard.

Indian Case 01 / 05
R.G. Anand v. Deluxe Films (1978)

This landmark Supreme Court decision established the idea–expression dichotomy as a foundational principle of Indian copyright law. The Court held that copyright does not subsist in ideas, themes, or plots, but only in their expression.

The Court introduced the ordinary observer test, asking whether an average viewer would perceive the later work as a copy of the original. Applying this test, the Court found no infringement, as the similarities were limited to general themes rather than specific expression.

Idea–ExpressionOrdinary Observer Test
Indian Case 02 / 05
Gramophone Company of India v. Super Cassette Industries (2010)

The Court adopted a strict approach to copyright protection in the context of music. It held that commercial use of copyrighted material without authorization constitutes infringement, regardless of the extent of modification.

This decision significantly restricted the scope of remixing and sampling in India, reinforcing that commercial intent eliminates any fair dealing protection that might otherwise apply.

Indian Case 03 / 05
Star India Pvt. Ltd. v. Leo Burnett (India) Pvt. Ltd.

The Court reinforced that even minor modifications of a copyrighted work require authorization. The decision emphasized that commercial use cannot be justified under fair dealing, regardless of degree of alteration.

This case further narrowed the permissible scope for creative reuse in commercial contexts and set a high threshold for what constitutes sufficient transformation under Indian law.

Indian Case 04 / 05
Civic Chandran v. Ammini Amma

The Kerala High Court recognised that parody and satire may fall within the scope of fair dealing under Indian law — a comparatively progressive position within the Indian framework.

However, the Court clarified that only limited portions of a work may be used for such purposes, and that parodic intent must be evident. The decision stops well short of the broad transformative use doctrine developed in the United States.

Indian Case 05 / 05
Sholay Copyright Case

The Delhi High Court reinforced the protection of cinematographic works and awarded damages for unauthorized use. The case confirmed that copyright protection continues to apply even to older, iconic works.

The decision serves as a reminder that cultural prominence of a work does not diminish its legal protection, and that unauthorized commercial exploitation carries significant financial consequences.

"Thoda Resham Lagta Hai" & "Addictive"

The song Thoda Resham Lagta Hai, composed by Bappi Lahiri, was later sampled internationally in Addictive by Truth Hurts. Saregama India initiated legal action against the unauthorized use of the track.

However, the situation revealed a layered complexity, as the original composition had itself drawn from earlier international work. This illustrates the difficulty of applying strict copyright principles in an industry shaped by iterative borrowing.

Industry Example 02 / 03
Black Eyed Peas & Asha Bhosle

The Black Eyed Peas incorporated elements from songs originally sung by Asha Bhosle into their production. The matter was resolved through licensing, reflecting the increasing importance of formal permissions in global music production.

This example demonstrates how rights-holder negotiations can resolve disputes that copyright litigation might have prolonged, while highlighting the asymmetry of bargaining power between global and regional creators.

Industry Example 03 / 03
Chuttamalle (2024) & Manike Mage Hithe

The 2024 controversy surrounding Chuttamalle, which drew comparisons with the Sri Lankan hit Manike Mage Hithe, illustrates the divergence between public perception, industry practice, and legal enforcement.

Despite noticeable similarities, no legal action followed, and the original composer acknowledged the resemblance positively. This highlights how informal norms often govern creative borrowing in practice, in contrast to formal legal mechanisms.

International Case 01 / 07
Campbell v. Acuff-Rose Music (1994)

A watershed ruling establishing that parody and transformative use are central to the fair use inquiry. The Supreme Court unanimously held that 2 Live Crew's parody of Roy Orbison's "Oh, Pretty Woman" could qualify as fair use.

The Court emphasized that commerciality does not automatically weigh against fair use, and that parody has inherent communicative value as social commentary.

International Case 02 / 07
Rogers v. Koons (1992)

Artist Jeff Koons reproduced a photograph by Art Rogers in a sculpture, claiming artistic transformation as a defence. The Second Circuit rejected this, holding that where transformation is minimal, the appropriation constitutes infringement.

The case underscores that artistic intent alone does not constitute a legal defence under fair use.

Appropriation ArtMinimal Transformation
International Case 03 / 07
Cariou v. Prince (2013)

Artist Richard Prince incorporated Cariou's photographs into large-scale paintings. The Second Circuit found that 25 of 30 works constituted fair use, employing a broad conception of transformation focused on visual aesthetics rather than commentary.

Critics argue this introduces excessive judicial subjectivity; supporters see it as necessary flexibility for contemporary art.

Transformative UseAppropriation Art
International Case 04 / 07
Feist Publications v. Rural Telephone Service (1991)

The Supreme Court held that mere labour ("sweat of the brow") is insufficient to establish copyright protection. Works must contain a minimal degree of creativity in their selection or arrangement.

This ruling established the constitutional requirement for originality under US law and remains foundational to understanding the boundaries between protectable expression and unprotectable facts.

OriginalityCreativity Threshold
International Case 05 / 07
Bridgeport Music v. Dimension Films (2005)

The Sixth Circuit adopted an absolute prohibition on digital music sampling without licence: "Get a licence or do not sample." Any unauthorized digital sampling — however minimal — constitutes infringement.

This strict approach has been criticized for impeding musical creativity and driving up licensing costs, standing in contrast to the more flexible fair use analysis available in other creative contexts.

International Case 06 / 07
Authors Guild v. Google (2015)

The Second Circuit affirmed that Google's large-scale digitisation of millions of books constituted fair use, recognising the transformative public utility of making works searchable without displaying full texts.

The case demonstrates how systemic societal benefit can be a factor favouring fair use, even in highly commercial contexts.

Mass DigitisationPublic Utility
International Case 07 / 07
Andy Warhol Foundation v. Goldsmith (2023)

The Supreme Court held that Warhol's silkscreen portraits derived from Goldsmith's photograph of Prince were not transformative in the relevant legal sense, given the commercial context and impact on licensing markets.

The decision signals a narrowing of transformative use doctrine and a re-emphasis on market harm, representing a significant recalibration of fair use analysis.

05 — Comparative Perspective

Jurisdictional Comparison

🇺🇸United States
Fair Use Doctrine

A flexible, multi-factor doctrine allowing courts to evaluate each case based on context, focusing on transformation and market impact. This flexibility enables adaptation to new forms of creativity and technological innovation.

Most flexible — highest judicial discretion
🇮🇳India
Fair Dealing (§52, CA 1957)

A closed-list fair dealing system providing certainty but limiting courts from accommodating emerging creative practices. Absence of transformative use doctrine and strict commercial intent rules create a restrictive environment.

Most restrictive — limited to listed purposes
🇬🇧United Kingdom
Narrow Fair Dealing

A similarly narrow approach but recognises certain exceptions such as parody. Provides marginally greater flexibility than India, though the framework remains significantly more limited than the American fair use approach.

Restrictive — with limited parody exception
🇪🇺European Union
Hybrid Model

Combines harmonized statutory exceptions across member states with evolving judicial interpretation. The Digital Single Market Directive reflects an attempt to modernize exceptions for the online environment.

Balanced — statutory with evolving interpretation
06 — Contemporary Issues

Emerging Tensions in the Digital Age

Generative AI systems are trained on large datasets that often include copyrighted works. This raises questions about whether such use constitutes infringement or falls within permissible exceptions. The lack of transparency in AI systems complicates this issue further.

This development challenges the foundational premise of copyright law as an incentive mechanism. If creativity can be generated through automated systems drawing on existing works, the traditional link between authorship and protection becomes increasingly attenuated.

Technological advancements have enabled the replication of voice, likeness, and artistic style. While copyright law does not traditionally protect style, these developments challenge existing legal boundaries.

A critical issue is whether stylistic imitation — distinct from direct copying — should attract copyright protection. Increasing sophistication of synthetic media challenges the idea–expression dichotomy in unprecedented ways.

Short-form content such as clips and reels involves partial reproduction of works. This complicates the application of the substantial similarity standard, as even brief excerpts may carry significant expressive value.

It raises whether copyright law should differentiate between enduring and ephemeral uses in digital environments where content is rapidly circulated and consumed across platforms.

Digital platforms increasingly enforce copyright through automated systems. These systems often operate without nuanced legal analysis, leading to over-enforcement and shifting adjudicatory power away from courts.

This reflects a structural shift wherein private platforms increasingly assume the role of adjudicating copyright disputes, displacing traditional judicial processes in favour of algorithmic governance.

The global circulation of content has intensified concerns about the use of cultural expressions without attribution. Copyright law, focused on individual authorship, struggles to address such collective concerns and the commodification of cultural heritage.

Existing frameworks are designed to protect individual creativity, not collective cultural traditions held by communities or indigenous peoples — a structural limitation that requires legislative attention.

Creative Commons and similar frameworks enable collaborative creativity but do not fully resolve tensions between ownership and accessibility. Their effectiveness depends on awareness, accessibility, and consistent application.

While offering a partial solution to the rigidity of copyright law, open licensing exists in a complementary rather than adversarial relationship with traditional copyright, representing a pragmatic path for creators seeking wider reach.

07 — Analysis

Critical Legal Analysis

"Copyright law operates within a fundamental tension between protection and innovation — excessive protection restricts creative experimentation, while insufficient protection undermines the rights and incentives of creators."

In the Indian context, the emphasis on strict enforcement and the absence of a transformative use doctrine create a legal environment that prioritises control over flexibility. While this protects copyright holders, it limits the scope for remix-based creativity and may discourage experimentation.

The doctrine of transformative use, as developed in the United States, attempts to balance these competing interests. However, its application remains subjective and context-dependent, introducing unpredictability into copyright adjudication.

A sustainable copyright framework must recognise transformation as a legitimate form of creativity while ensuring that original works are not exploited without authorization. The 2023 Warhol decision signals a potential course correction — pulling back from expansive transformation and re-emphasising market harm.

Whether this recalibration adequately serves contemporary creative culture — particularly as AI, platform governance, and global content flows reshape artistic practice — remains an open and deeply contested question.

08 — Creator Guide

Practical Guidance for Creators

✓
Permissible Engagement

Use of copyrighted material is generally allowed for criticism, review, and parody, provided it does not involve substantial reproduction. Ensure your use falls within statutory exceptions applicable in your jurisdiction.

⚠
Legal Risks

Commercial use of existing material without authorization carries significant legal risk. Avoid using recognizable portions of a work in a manner that substitutes the original or competes with it in the same market.

✦
Best Practices

Where reliance on existing material is substantial, obtaining licences is the most reliable course of action. Attribution, while not always legally sufficient, remains an important ethical practice.

⬡
Ethical Considerations

Respect cultural contexts and avoid exploitative appropriation of indigenous or community-held creative traditions. Legal permissibility does not always equate to ethical legitimacy — both dimensions deserve consideration.

09 — Conclusion

The Road Ahead

Copyright law was designed for a context in which creative works were produced independently and reproduced in identifiable forms. Contemporary creativity, however, is increasingly collaborative, iterative, and technologically mediated.

"The distinction between originality and derivation has become less clear, requiring a more nuanced legal approach. A rigid framework risks inhibiting innovation, while an overly permissive system risks undermining the rights of creators."

The future of copyright law lies in its ability to adapt to changing creative practices while maintaining its foundational objective of promoting creativity. This demands renewed engagement between legislators, courts, creators, and technologists — to forge a framework that is both principled and responsive.
